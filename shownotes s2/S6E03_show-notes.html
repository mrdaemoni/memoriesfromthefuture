Supporting Synthesis

Episode Summary

The third koan emerged in a sauna — the realization that mastery doesn't expand outward but compresses inward. Leonard's plateaus, Ericsson's deliberate practice, and the neuroscience of myelination all point to the same paradox: the spiral looks like repetition from outside, but from inside, each pass changes the passer.

Thinkers Featured

George Leonard

20th c. — Journalism / Aikido

The graph of mastery is long plateaus with brief spurts. Most people quit on the plateau, not realizing the plateau IS the practice.

K. Anders Ericsson

20th–21st c. — Psychology

Deliberate practice. Not 10,000 hours of repetition, but 10,000 hours of returning to what is not yet right, under conditions of difficulty, while actively attending.

Ann Graybiel

20th–21st c. — Neuroscience

Basal ganglia, habit formation, myelination. The neural basis of how repeated practice physically restructures the brain, making circuits faster and more efficient. The spiral has a substrate.
